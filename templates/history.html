History of the Fair

The Road to the White City

1889

The Competition Begins

Congress announces a competition to select the host city for the 1893 World's Fair, commemorating the 400th anniversary of Columbus's discovery of America. Chicago, New York, Washington D.C., and St. Louis all submit proposals.

1890

Chicago Wins the Bid

After intense lobbying and a $10 million fundraising campaign led by Chicago's business elite, Congress awards the fair to Chicago. The city pledges to transform Jackson Park into the most magnificent exposition the world has ever seen.

1891

The Master Plan Takes Shape

Daniel Burnham is appointed Director of Works, and Frederick Law Olmsted designs the landscape. The "White City" concept emerges, featuring neoclassical architecture and a grand Court of Honor surrounding the Grand Basin.

1892

Construction Begins

Thousands of workers begin the monumental task of transforming Jackson Park. The Ferris Wheel, designed by George Washington Gale Ferris Jr., is approved as the fair's centerpiece attraction. The Midway Plaisance is developed as an entertainment district.

1893

The Fair Opens

On May 1st, President Grover Cleveland officially opens the World's Columbian Exposition. The "White City" dazzles visitors with its electric lights, innovative exhibits, and architectural grandeur. The fair runs for six months, welcoming over 27 million visitors.

Visionaries and Leaders

Daniel Burnham

As Director of Works, Burnham orchestrated the construction of the fair, coordinating hundreds of architects, artists, and workers. His famous quote, "Make no little plans," embodied the fair's ambitious vision.

person Director of Works

Frederick Law Olmsted

The renowned landscape architect designed the fairgrounds, creating a harmonious balance between grand architecture and natural beauty with his lagoons, wooded islands, and carefully planned vistas.

park Landscape Architect

Bertha Palmer

As President of the Board of Lady Managers, Palmer championed women's contributions to the fair, overseeing the Woman's Building and ensuring female representation in the exposition's planning and execution.

group President, Board of Lady Managers

George Washington Gale Ferris Jr.

The engineer who designed the iconic Ferris Wheel, which became the fair's most famous attraction. His 264-foot wheel could carry over 2,000 passengers and offered breathtaking views of the White City.

engineering Ferris Wheel Designer

The Fair's Enduring Legacy

The World's Columbian Exposition not only showcased American innovation and culture but also left an indelible mark on the nation's consciousness. The fair introduced millions to new technologies, artistic movements, and cultural perspectives that would shape the coming century.
